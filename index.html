Mathematical Foundations of Reinforcement Learning & Online Decision Making

A rigorous treatment of the mathematics underlying modern reinforcement learning — from Markov chain theory and stochastic approximation through bandit algorithms and regret analysis. By Sindri Magnússon.

Sindri Magnússon

About this course

What you will study

  • Markov chains, hitting times, mixing times
  • Stochastic approximation & convergence
  • Markov Decision Processes (finite & average horizon)
  • TD-learning, Q-learning, actor-critic methods
  • Bandit algorithms & sharp regret bounds

Approach

Every topic is developed from first principles. Algorithms are introduced alongside their convergence proofs. By the end, students can read and critique contemporary theory papers from venues like NeurIPS and ICML.

Who should attend

PhD students in machine learning, control theory, operations research, and applied mathematics. Advanced master's students with strong mathematical preparation are welcome.

Format

Ten lectures (roughly 90 minutes each), four substantial problem sets, a take-home exam, and a closing workshop where each participant presents a recent ICML or NeurIPS paper of their choosing.

Bandit lectures in depth

The final four lectures build a coherent theory of sequential decision-making under uncertainty. Each lecture is self-contained and proof-complete — you will leave knowing not just what the bounds are, but why they cannot be improved.

Lecture 08
Linear Stochastic Bandits

Rewards are linear in a known feature vector, with an unknown parameter θ*. We derive LinUCB from first principles, using ridge regression and the self-normalised matrix Bernstein inequality to obtain confidence ellipsoids that tighten with every pull.

  • Exploration–exploitation as an optimism principle
  • Ridge regression & elliptical potential lemma
  • Self-normalised concentration (Abbasi-Yadkori et al.)
  • Regret bound Õ(d√T) derivation in full
  • Thompson Sampling for linear models
Primary reading

Lattimore & Szepesvári, Bandit Algorithms, Chapters 19–20.
Abbasi-Yadkori, Pál, Szepesvári (2011). Improved algorithms for linear stochastic bandits. NeurIPS.

Lecture 09
Gap-Index Frameworks & Sample Complexity

When the goal is to identify the best arm rather than maximise cumulative reward, the right currency is sample complexity. We study the LUCB algorithm and provide a complete proof of its instance-dependent upper bound, then match it with an information-theoretic lower bound via Kullback–Leibler divergence.

  • Pure exploration vs. regret minimisation
  • LUCB algorithm and correctness proof
  • Gap-index sampling rule, arm elimination
  • Full proof: sample complexity O(∑ᵢ Δᵢ⁻² log(K/δ))
  • KL-based lower bounds (Kaufmann–Cappé–Garivier)
Primary reading

Lattimore & Szepesvári, Chapters 16–17.
Kaufmann, Cappé, Garivier (2016). On the complexity of best arm identification in multi-armed bandit models. JMLR.

Lecture 10
Restless Bandits

In restless bandits, arm states evolve as Markov chains even when not pulled — a model capturing spectrum allocation, clinical trials, and web crawling. We derive the Whittle relaxation, prove the indexability condition, and establish the asymptotic optimality of the index policy.

  • RMAB problem formulation & POMDP connection
  • LP relaxation and Whittle's decomposition
  • Indexability: definition & verification
  • Whittle index computation
  • Weber–Weiss asymptotic optimality theorem
Primary reading

Whittle (1988). Restless bandits: Activity allocation in a changing world. J. Applied Probability.
Weber & Weiss (1990). On an index policy for restless bandits. J. Applied Probability.
Niño-Mora (2007). Dynamic priority allocation via restless bandit marginal productivity indices. TOP.

Lecture 11
Related Bandit Families

A panoramic view of how the core bandit ideas extend: to adversarial environments (EXP3), to structured action sets (combinatorial bandits), to function spaces (Gaussian process bandits), and to sequential recommendations (cascading bandits).

  • Contextual bandits: policy classes & oracle reductions
  • EXP3 for adversarial bandits, minimax regret
  • GP-UCB and kernel methods, Srinivas et al.
  • Combinatorial bandits: matroid & path structures
  • Cascading bandits & position-based models
Primary reading

Lattimore & Szepesvári, Chapters 11, 24–25.
Srinivas et al. (2010). Gaussian process optimization in the bandit setting. ICML.
Kveton et al. (2015). Tight regret bounds for stochastic combinatorial semi-bandits. AISTATS.

Assessment

Problem Sets

Four substantial homework assignments, each released after roughly two lectures. Problems mix proof-writing, algorithm analysis, and numerical experiments. Collaboration is encouraged; write-ups must be individual.

HW 1 Markov chains — mixing times, coupling, convergence rates
HW 2 Stochastic approximation and MDP algorithms
HW 3 Reinforcement learning — TD-learning and Q-learning analysis
HW 4 Bandit algorithms — regret proofs and sample complexity

Take-home exam

A 48-hour take-home exam at the end of term. Open-book. Problems require synthesis across multiple topics covered in the course.

Closing workshop

Each student presents a recent ICML or NeurIPS paper relevant to the course material. Presentations are 20 minutes with 10 minutes Q&A. Papers may be suggested or chosen freely.
